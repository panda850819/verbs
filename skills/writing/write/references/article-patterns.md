## Chase Wang - AI is Here. And Who Am I Now?

- **Source**: chasewang2026.substack.com (2025)
- **Type**: Opinion/essay with data backing

### Techniques
1. **Multi-persona hook**: Opens with 5 different reader scenarios (student, founder, CEO, lawyer, creator) -- reader self-selects into one, instant resonance
2. **Validate before advising**: Spends ~40% of article establishing "the fear is real" with data before offering any reframe
3. **Single framework spine**: Erikson's identity crisis model unifies the entire piece -- every section maps back to it
4. **Historical pattern proof**: 4 historical parallels (printing press, industrial revolution, photography, GPS) each following the same 4-stage pattern
5. **Segmented advice**: Separate actionable sections for each audience (students, founders, enterprise, experts, creators)
6. **Vulnerability before close**: Second-to-last section admits "not everyone will come out ahead" -- honesty increases trust right before the inspirational ending
7. **Bilingual anchor**: Closes with Chinese proverb (凡是過往，皆為序章) bridging cultural resonance

### Structure
```
1. Hook -- 5 personas, same underlying question [Task: make reader feel seen]
2. Data section -- stats proving anxiety is real [Task: validate the fear]
3. Reframe -- "this is an identity crisis, not a tech problem" [Task: shift the lens]
4. Historical parallels -- 4 cases, same pattern [Task: prove the framework]
5. The pattern -- explicit 4-stage model [Task: give reader a mental model]
6. Advice by segment -- students/founders/enterprise/experts/creators [Task: actionable takeaways]
7. Honesty section -- "not everyone wins" [Task: earn trust]
8. Closing -- Shakespeare + Chinese, past is prologue [Task: emotional resolution]
```

### Best For
- Long-form opinion pieces addressing broad anxiety/change
- Articles targeting multiple audience segments
- When you need to convince skeptical readers (data-first approach)

## Ping Chen (artistic709) - Ethereum 10Y Review

- **Source**: artistic709.medium.com (2026-03)
- **Type**: Industry retrospective / personal reflection

### Techniques
1. **Physical object anchor**: Opens with a GPU warranty sticker dated 2016 -- a tangible, specific, unchallengeable detail that grounds "ten years" in reality instead of abstraction
2. **Numbers as confession**: "50 classmates, 10+ in AI, 3 in blockchain" -- lets data deliver the uncomfortable truth so the author doesn't have to editorialize
3. **Borrowed frameworks, twisted**: Uses Tang Feng's Fork-and-Merge, information economics signaling theory, USB Type-C standard wars -- each borrowed from a completely different domain, then bent to fit blockchain's story. The cross-domain jumps feel like a real person's associative thinking, not a structured argument
4. **Self-undermining honesty**: "I don't particularly like Solana, but OPOS is a good slogan" / "I don't have a good answer yet" -- states preferences then immediately qualifies them. Never pretends to have resolved his own contradictions
5. **Dark humor section titles**: "史前朋友發現火了，發爐" (cavemen discovered fire and went furnace) / "鏈還在，人沒了" (chain's still here, people aren't) -- titles do emotional work that the body text doesn't repeat
6. **Corpse-mapping metaphor**: "We mapped the river's terrain by observing where the bodies fell" -- a single grotesque image that replaces an entire paragraph of "we learned from failure"
7. **Wordplay as argument**: "區塊鏈原生" → "區塊鏈往生" (blockchain native → blockchain deceased) -- the pun IS the thesis of that section, not decoration
8. **Utility-as-water thesis**: "Think of trust like tap water -- how it's produced, purchased, stockpiled, consumed, and generates commercial value" -- forces reader to re-examine an abstract concept through industrial economics
9. **Circular close**: Ends with buying a new GPU, mirroring the warranty sticker opening. But the mood has shifted from nostalgia to uncertainty -- same object, different weight
10. **Uneven pacing**: Some sections are dense argument (信任經濟學), others are one-paragraph vibes (鏈還在人沒了). The rhythm variation keeps attention and signals which parts the author cares most about

### Structure
```
1. Physical anchor -- warranty sticker, "ten years" [Task: ground the timeline in something real]
2. Current state -- class ratio, industry position [Task: honest assessment without drama]
3. Middle hollowing -- what's left between money and ideology [Task: name the loss]
4. Fork and Merge -- borrowed framework [Task: reframe "selling out" as natural process]
5. Credible neutral -- the surviving value prop [Task: answer "why blockchain still"]
6. Public sea metaphor -- USB-C, standards wars [Task: make the abstract concrete]
7. Information economics -- trust as commodity [Task: theoretical spine for "why this matters"]
8. Three eras -- 2015-19 / 2020-23 / 2024+ [Task: historical context, but only enough to serve the argument]
9. Two futures -- idealist vs pragmatist paths [Task: honest fork without false resolution]
10. Circular close -- new GPU, new sticker [Task: emotional landing, open-ended]
```

### Key Structural Insight
The piece is NOT chronological even though it covers ten years. It opens with present (sections 1-3), zooms out to theory (4-7), then does history (8), then future (9-10). History is buried in the back third -- used as evidence, not as the spine. This prevents the "and then, and then, and then" trap of most retrospectives.

### Best For
- Personal industry retrospectives (especially "is this field still worth it" pieces)
- Writing about decline or ambiguity without being either bitter or falsely optimistic
- Long-form where the author's lived experience IS the credential
- Pieces that need to hold multiple contradictions without resolving them

## Ping Chen - Cross-Article Voice Analysis (5 articles)

- **Source**: artistic709.medium.com (2019-2026)
- **Type**: Author voice profile -- patterns consistent across all articles

### Signature Moves (appear in 3+ articles)

1. **Analogy from a completely different universe**: Every article borrows at least one framework from outside crypto. Hackathon piece: iPhone App Store maturity cycle, 蝦皮/Momo e-commerce. ETH price: PoW/PoS math meets supply-demand curves. Polymarket: 堂島米會所 (1730 Osaka rice futures), Icarus myth. 10Y Review: USB-C standard wars, information economics signaling. He never explains crypto with crypto -- always with something the reader already understands from daily life or history.

2. **One-liner verdicts that replace paragraphs**: "不要玩火" (don't play with fire) -- 4 characters that are the entire thesis of the Polymarket piece. "人走茶涼" (people left, tea went cold). "區塊鏈原生→區塊鏈往生". He drops these like anchors, then the surrounding paragraphs exist to support the one-liner, not the other way around.

3. **Food/physical comfort as tonal reset**: "不變的是披薩炸雞無限量供應，所以你怎能不愛黑客松？" inserted between serious paragraphs about industry maturity. In the Hakka piece: "awesome T-shirt, too bad they ran out before I can get one". These aren't random -- they break tension at exactly the right moment and remind you a real person is writing.

4. **Problem-Solution-But-Actually structure**: BlackHoleSwap solved infinite liquidity -- but MakerDAO fixed the underlying problem, making the solution worthless. 3F Mutual solved DeFi insurance -- but only for black swan events. RWA hackathon solved real-world problems -- but lost the wild innovation spirit. The "but" is never a failure admission -- it's intellectual honesty about how solutions interact with moving contexts.

5. **Doesn't resolve his own contradictions**: "我還沒有很好的答案" (I don't have a good answer yet). "哪個方向更好，我說不上來" (which direction is better, I can't say). He presents forks and leaves them forked. This is the anti-AI-slop move -- AI always resolves to a tidy conclusion.

6. **Bureaucratic/legal detail as narrative texture**: Polymarket piece names "雲林地檢署 70 名人力", "5501.98 顆 USDC", specific legal codes. Hackathon piece names "清華大學主辦、imToken 協辦". These ultra-specific institutional details signal "I was actually there" without saying so.

### Writing Modes by Article Type

| Type | Example | Key Pattern |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Industry retrospective** | 10Y Review | Non-chronological structure, physical object anchor, circular close |
| **Technical economics** | ETH Tech Bad for Price | Counterintuitive thesis stated upfront, math kept to minimum viable, self-deprecating close ("還是再買點 ETH 吧") |
| **Legal/policy analysis** | Polymarket I | Historical precedent chain (堂島→Iowa→台灣), "don't do this" thesis before explaining why, myth/fable close (Icarus) |
| **Event reflection** | RWA Hackathon | Era comparison (大航海→大都市), industry lifecycle analogy (iPhone App Store), personal anecdote close (Kyber hackathon memory) |
| **Project narrative** | Hakka Finance and I | Honest post-mortem per product (worked→then didn't→why), open questions about DAO governance left unanswered |

### Anti-Patterns (things he never does)

- Never uses "在這個快速變化的時代" or any AI-opener equivalent
- Never lists "3 key takeaways" or "5 things to remember"
- Never ends with an inspirational call-to-action
- Never claims a clean success story -- every win has a "but"
- Never explains what blockchain is to the reader (assumes shared context)
- Never uses 賦能/閉環/抓手/底層邏輯 (Chinese AI-slop vocabulary)

### Sentence-Level Rhythm

- **Short thought units**: Rarely more than 2 clauses per sentence in Chinese
- **Paragraph length varies wildly**: Some are 1 sentence, some are 8. The variation IS the rhythm
- **English tech terms stay English**: DeFi, TVL, PoS, OPOS, Fork and Merge -- never awkwardly translated
- **Decisive endings**: Sentences end with 。not ⋯ (except when genuinely uncertain, then ⋯ is used sparingly and deliberately)
- **Parenthetical asides as personality**: "(第一次評黑客松，辛苦程度不輸參賽)", "(awesome T-shirt, too bad they ran out)" -- voice leaks through parentheses

### Technical Writing Mode (3 articles analyzed)

Ping Chen's technical articles have a distinct voice separate from his opinion pieces:

#### Techniques

1. **Absurd-image explanation**: Instead of dry technical analogies, uses vivid/grotesque images to explain mechanisms. Liquidity Auction: "把一群羊圈起來，拍賣幫他們剃羊毛的機會" (pen up the sheep, auction off the right to shear them). LP token = fence, AMM curve = scissors, bid price = pasture. The metaphor is complete and internally consistent, not just decorative.

2. **Problem stated as a paradox**: Never "here's a problem, here's a solution". Instead frames it as a genuine contradiction. "AMM solves the agency problem but creates the externality problem" / "散戶參與造市有著本質上難解的矛盾" (retail market-making has an inherently irreconcilable contradiction). The paradox IS the hook.

3. **Extreme scenarios to clarify**: Uses boundary conditions as thought experiments. "假設有一個零手續費的 Uniswap，做 LP 絕對只會賠錢。反之，假設有一個善心人士總是願意用市價調倉，那 LP 穩賺不賠" -- two impossible extremes that perfectly bracket the real problem.

4. **Math stays high-school level**: Even in deeply technical pieces (YieldSpace AMM, PoS issuance), math never goes beyond `sqrt()`, basic algebra, and supply-demand curves. Complex ideas are conveyed through intuition-building, not notation. The Fixed Interest Rate guide explicitly uses "complex but understandable" as a design principle.

5. **Honest post-mortems inline**: BlackHoleSwap "had its moment" -- 100M USD trading volume in month one, then the underlying problem (DAI depeg) was fixed by MakerDAO and the solution became worthless. He doesn't spin this. "The trading volume proves this solution works, but with the problem gone, the solution was worthless." Zero cope.

6. **Naming as strategy, openly discussed**: "The first thing about my DeFi ecosystem is its name which should be trendy and catchy" / whitepaper designed to be "complex but understandable" so people feel smart sharing it. He exposes the marketing mechanics of his own products without embarrassment.

7. **Gas Efficient Card Drawing structure**: The NFT card-drawing piece is a masterclass in technical brevity. Scenario → Spec → Algorithm → Tweak → Performance. No preamble, no "in this article we will". Opens with the creator's preference ("isn't a fan of blind boxes"), states the spec in 3 sentences, then solves it. Total word count is ~800.

#### Technical Structure Patterns

| Pattern | Example | When to Use |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Paradox → Mechanism → Game Theory** | Liquidity Auction | Novel DeFi primitive -- need to convince skeptics |
| **Scenario → Spec → Algorithm → Tweak → Limits** | Card Drawing | Pure engineering solution -- readers want code |
| **Survey → Taxonomy → Comparison → Future** | Fixed Interest Rate Guide | Landscape analysis -- readers need mental map |
| **Timeline → Product → Honest Result → Next** | Hakka Finance and I | Project narrative -- readers want the real story |

#### Anti-Patterns in Technical Writing

- Never opens with "In this article, we will explore..."
- Never ends with "In conclusion, X is an exciting field with much potential"
- Never hides failures -- states them as matter-of-fact data points
- Never over-qualifies obvious statements ("it could potentially perhaps")
- Never separates "technical content" from "personality" -- the sheep metaphor IS the technical explanation

### How to Use This Profile

When sparring or editing, check against these patterns:

**For opinion/retrospective pieces:**
- Is there at least one cross-domain analogy? (from outside the article's topic)
- Is there a one-liner verdict that could stand alone?
- Does the piece resolve too neatly? (Ping Chen wouldn't)
- Are there physical/sensory details grounding the abstract?
- Does the rhythm vary between sections, or is it monotone?

**For technical pieces:**
- Is the core problem framed as a paradox or contradiction?
- Are extreme/boundary scenarios used to build intuition?
- Is the math kept to minimum viable complexity?
- Are there concrete numbers (gas costs, TVL, trading volume) as evidence?
- Does the conclusion honestly state limitations?
- Is there at least one vivid metaphor that IS the explanation, not decoration?

Reference as "Ping Chen style" or "artistic709 pattern" during `/write spar` or `/write edit`.

## zodchiii - 10 Claude Workflows That Save Me 10+ Hours a Week

- **Source**: x.com/zodchiii/status/2037091952328663230 (2026-03)
- **Type**: Personal productivity listicle with data backing

### Techniques

1. **Tracked data as credibility**: Two weeks of actual time tracking before writing. Numbers aren't estimates — they're logged. "I have been tracking my Claude usage for 2 weeks straight. Timestamps, tasks, time saved vs doing it manually." The tracking method IS the authority.
2. **Before/After table as proof**: Instead of "saves time," presents a structured comparison table (Workflow / Before / After / Saved per week). Reader calculates ROI themselves — no persuasion needed.
3. **"Why this line exists" teaching**: Each prompt template includes an explanation of the critical line. "The line that makes this work is 'If data is insufficient, say so.' Without it Claude confidently invents numbers." Shares the scar, not just the solution.
4. **Meta-insight at the end that reframes everything**: 10 workflows are the content, but the real thesis lands in the final section: "Most of what I called 'working' was really context-switching." One sentence that makes the reader rethink the entire premise — it was never about AI, it was about attention.
5. **Caveat as trust-builder**: "These numbers are mine. Your mileage will vary." / "Is it perfect? No. It misses things sometimes." Honest qualifications placed after the data, not before — so they read as confidence, not hedging.

### Structure

```
1. Claim -- specific number in title [Task: set expectation, filterable]
2. Setup -- tools used, no API/no code [Task: lower barrier, widen audience]
3. Workflows x10 -- each: problem → old way → prompt → why it works [Task: repeatable value]
4. The Math -- full tracking table [Task: prove the claim]
5. Meta-insight -- "it's context-switching, not productivity" [Task: reframe, make reader think]
```

### Key Structural Insight

The listicle format is flat (each workflow has equal weight), but the piece avoids boredom by varying section length and embedding "old way" mini-narratives that readers recognize from their own experience. The real payload is section 5 — the meta-insight — which elevates it from "tips list" to "perspective shift."

### Best For

- "How I use tool X" personal workflow articles
- Productivity content that needs data credibility over opinion
- When you have tracked metrics to back claims

### What NOT to Copy

- Flat listicle structure (no hierarchy between items) — works for Twitter threads, weak for long-form blog
- Repeated CTA insertions (TG channel plugged twice) — growth hack, not voice
- Prompt templates without context on when they fail — zodchiii partially addresses this, but some workflows are presented as magic bullets

## Paweł Huryn - The Claude Dispatch Guide: 48 Hours Running AI From My Phone

- **Source**: x.com/PawelHuryn/status/2036058594433519790 (2026-03)
- **Type**: Product review / personal workflow narrative with real-world testing

### Techniques

1. **Scene-embedded feature demo**: Instead of listing features, embeds them in daily life scenes. "Walking the dog. Check Task 1 on my phone — competitor summary done." Reader imagines themselves in the scenario, not reading a spec sheet. Each scene is one paragraph, one moment, one capability.
2. **Systematic gotcha section**: Dedicates an entire section to friction points, each with a workaround or explicit "no workaround yet." Not buried as disclaimers — elevated to a first-class section. Signals: "I actually used this, not just demoed it."
3. **Contribution split table**: "Thinking: 90% me. Research and formatting: 90% Claude." Explicit human/AI role breakdown replaces vague "AI helped me." Reader knows exactly what was delegated and what required judgment.
4. **Public self-correction**: "Two corrections since the first wave of Dispatch coverage on X. Getting things wrong publicly and fixing them publicly is part of how this works." Admits prior errors in the article itself, not in a separate post. Turns vulnerability into methodology.
5. **Reframe at the end**: The 48-hour test isn't about filling dead time — it's about restructuring your day because work runs without you sitting in front of it. Same move as zodchiii's context-switching insight: the meta-observation is more valuable than the tips.
6. **Decision framework table**: "When to use Dispatch vs. Web Sessions vs. Channels vs. Code" — gives reader a quick lookup instead of making them read 4 separate guides. Respects reader's time.

### Structure

```
1. What it is (and isn't) -- clear definition [Task: set expectations, prevent confusion]
2. Setup steps -- numbered, actionable [Task: remove friction to trying]
3. 48-hour timeline -- scenes from daily life [Task: show real usage, not demo usage]
4. Gotchas -- systematic friction documentation [Task: build trust, save reader time]
5. Comparison table -- when to use which surface [Task: decision framework]
6. Knowledge layer -- why the system compounds [Task: strategic insight beyond the tool]
7. Contribution split + meta-insight [Task: reframe the entire premise]
```

### Key Structural Insight

The piece uses temporal narrative (48-hour timeline) as its backbone, but the actual value is in sections 4-7 which are non-temporal. The timeline hooks you in ("what happened"), the analysis sections deliver the real payload ("what it means"). This hybrid of narrative + framework is stronger than either alone.

### Best For

- AI tool reviews based on real usage periods (not first impressions)
- Product comparisons where multiple options exist
- When you have genuine friction/gotcha data to share
- "I tested X for Y days" format articles

### What NOT to Copy

- PM-centric framing (orchestrator, workstreams, stakeholders) — too narrow for builder audience
- Multiple CTA links to paid content throughout the article
- Some sections repeat the same point (knowledge layer compounds) without adding new evidence

## Alan Chan (詹雨安) - Heptabase Founder Blog (~60 articles, 2015-2026)

- **Source**: sheracaolity.ghost.io + alanchan1209.medium.com
- **Type**: Cross-article voice analysis -- patterns from founder/learning essays spanning 10 years

### Signature Moves (appear across majority of articles)

1. **先講結論 (Conclusion First)**: Nearly every article opens with the thesis or conclusion, then unpacks reasoning. "先講結論：做產品應該像養小孩一樣。" / "一旦解法的形狀被找出來了，剩下的就僅僅是計算。" The reader never has to guess where the article is going. This is the anti-clickbait move -- respects reader time.

2. **Cross-domain framework borrowing**: His most distinctive intellectual move. In a single article about molecular storage technology, he pulls Kurzweil's paradigm shift theory, Christensen's disruptive innovation, S-curves, and brain-computer interface speculation -- each from a completely different field. Unlike Ping Chen's one-liner analogies, Alan's borrowings are full-framework transplants that get 3-5 paragraphs of development.

3. **Growth-as-proof writing**: Uses his own data as evidence without bragging. "MRR grew 40x in 3 months at YC" / "Iterated through 300+ versions" / "Team of 6, cash-flow positive since year one." Numbers appear as matter-of-fact data points, never as hype.

4. **Self-deprecating retrospection**: "回頭看自己以前寫的東西時常會感到羞恥" (I often feel ashamed reading what I wrote before). Uses past embarrassment as proof of growth. Never pretends to have always known what he now understands.

5. **Series writing as real-time documentation**: 休學系列 (Drop-out series, 序~九), 創業筆記 (Startup Notes, 四~六), London Relocation (Visa/Rent/Taipei vs London). Each installment captures the thinking AT THAT STAGE, not reconstructed afterwards. The value compounds across entries.

6. **Bilingual mirror articles**: Many articles exist in both Chinese and English versions (Heptabase 1.0, Acquire Knowledge, Use Cases). Not translations -- each version is written natively for its audience. English versions tend to be more structured/formal; Chinese versions more conversational.

7. **Short paragraphs, decisive endings**: Sentences end with 。not ⋯ or ...。Paragraphs rarely exceed 4-5 sentences. Lists and numbered structures appear frequently but feel organic, not forced. Very few hedging words.

8. **Framework as spine**: Nearly every substantial article has ONE borrowed framework that holds the entire piece together. Erikson identity crisis (2025 retrospective), S-curves (molecule article), "product = child raising" metaphor, YC's "Startup = Growth" definition. The framework is stated early and every section maps back to it.

### Writing Modes by Article Type

| Type | Key Pattern | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Startup note** | Real-time lesson + specific data + "here's what I got wrong" | 創業筆記(六): 聚焦與招聘 |
| **Learning methodology** | Step-by-step method + visual thinking emphasis + "the real insight is..." | Acquire Knowledge from Readings |
| **Product vision** | Problem → positioned solution → what we chose NOT to do → roadmap | Heptabase 1.0, vs Notion vs Obsidian |
| **Personal reflection** | Growth trajectory + past shame + future aspiration | 學習軌跡, 2025 年回顧 |
| **Book/concept review** | Framework extraction + cross-domain application + original example | 淺談分子存儲技術, 《從零到一》 |
| **Life documentation** | Honest observation + specific detail + no forced lesson | London Relocation, 布丁湯匙, 雙螢幕 |

### Sentence-Level Rhythm

- **Chinese**: Conversational but not sloppy. Tech terms stay English (PMF, ARR, MRR, HC, Inbox). Sentences average 20-30 characters. Frequent use of 「引號」for emphasis and direct speech.
- **English**: More structured/formal than his Chinese. Longer sentences. But still direct -- no filler openings.
- **Transition style**: Minimal transitions between paragraphs. Jumps directly to next point. Relies on clear section headers rather than transitional phrases.
- **Parenthetical personality**: Occasional parentheticals that leak personality: "(可能他也很想多踢足球吧)", "(結果做了之後才發現比想像中困難)"

### Anti-Patterns (things he never does)

- Never uses 賦能/閉環/抓手/底層邏輯 (Chinese AI slop)
- Never opens with "在這個快速變化的時代"
- Never writes a conclusion that merely restates the introduction
- Never hides failures or sanitizes mistakes
- Never writes without specific data/examples (even personal reflections have concrete numbers)
- Never forces a happy ending -- articles about difficulty end with difficulty acknowledged
- Never writes filler: if a section can be a single paragraph, it is

### Evolution Across 10 Years

The blog is a living document of intellectual growth:
- **2015-2017** (高中/大學): Movie reviews, course summaries, football dreams. Writing is enthusiastic but narrow. "活在自己的小世界裡頭" by his own admission.
- **2018-2020** (Minerva/休學): Cross-domain thinking begins. 分子存儲技術, 孫子兵法, 人類大歷史. Still somewhat derivative of books read.
- **2021-2023** (Heptabase 0→1): Startup notes become the main output. Product thinking sharpens. Writing becomes more disciplined, data-driven.
- **2024-2026** (Heptabase 1→10): Philosophical maturity. 不確定性, 道路, Twelve Enduring Questions. Comfortable with ambiguity. Shorter, more distilled pieces.

### How to Use This Profile

**For sparring/structuring Panda's articles:**
- Does the piece open with its conclusion? (Alan's 先講結論 pattern)
- Is there a single framework holding the piece together?
- Are there specific numbers/data as evidence, not just opinions?
- Does the piece honestly acknowledge what you got wrong or didn't know?
- Are cross-domain connections present? (Not just within the topic's field)

**For editing:**
- Check paragraph length -- should rarely exceed 4-5 sentences
- Tech terms should stay in English
- Transitions should be minimal -- let section headers do the work
- Look for hedging language to eliminate
- Any conclusion that restates the intro needs a twist or new insight

**When to reference:** Use "Alan Chan style" when writing startup reflections, learning methodology articles, product vision pieces, or personal growth essays. Especially relevant for pieces that need intellectual framework + personal honesty.

## Panda - 回顧式月報 (很忙，但好像什麼都還沒做)

- **Source**: Blog/monthly/ (2026-04)
- **Type**: Personal retrospective / monthly review with narrative flow

### Techniques

1. **Narrative flow over section isolation**: Sections exist but connect to each other through transitional sentences ("做完 X 之後，我開始想 Y"). No `---` horizontal rules. Each topic leads naturally to the next, like someone sitting down and thinking back through the month.
2. **One topic per paragraph**: Never cram multiple subjects into one paragraph. When the subject shifts (even slightly), start a new paragraph. Dense paragraphs kill the retrospective rhythm.
3. **Numbers embedded in story, not tables**: Instead of markdown tables, weave data into narrative prose. "94 個 skills 砍到 83 個再砍到 23 個" reads better than a table. Reserve tables only for data that truly needs comparison.
4. **Dates as texture, not timestamps**: Use "二月中", "三月初", "某天凌晨" instead of "2/13", "3/25". The retrospective voice remembers feelings and sequences, not exact dates.
5. **Title as closing line**: The title restates as the final sentence (as a blockquote), creating a circular structure. No additional conclusion needed. The reflection sections before it do the emotional work.
6. **Honest self-diagnosis in the final section**: The last major section before closing isn't a to-do list but a genuine examination of underlying patterns and anxieties. Frame it as discovery, not confession.
7. **生活 section with personal voice**: Life events told as bullet-point anecdotes with personality (parenthetical asides, humor). Not a flat timeline but small stories.
8. **有趣的事與物 as curated appendix**: A pseudoyu-inspired section at the very end, organized by category (文章/工具/影片/Crypto/值得關注的人), each item with a one-line reason why it's interesting. Functions as a newsletter appendix.
9. **Data visualization cards**: Key data points (e.g., 954 notes vs 0 published, skill count reduction) rendered as dark-themed PNG cards for X/social sharing. Dramatic typography, before/after contrast.

### Structure

```
1. Opening -- conversational re-entry, what happened in summary [Task: set the tone, show self-awareness]
2. Project sections (2-3) -- narrative flow, each connecting to the next [Task: show what was built/explored]
3. Knowledge/research section -- input volume vs output gap [Task: honest accounting]
4. Work + trading (if applicable) -- brief, operational [Task: completeness without oversharing]
5. 生活 -- personal anecdotes with voice [Task: remind reader a real person is writing]
6. Reflection/next month -- self-diagnosis of underlying patterns [Task: depth, not planning]
7. Title callback as closing line [Task: circular structure, emotional landing]
8. 有趣的事與物 -- curated links appendix [Task: share value beyond the personal narrative]
```

### Key Structural Insight

The piece is NOT a status report. It's a retrospective where the author discovers something about themselves through the act of reviewing. The structure moves from "what I did" (sections 1-5) to "what it means" (section 6) to "what I still don't know" (closing). The 有趣的事與物 appendix is deliberately placed AFTER the emotional close, functioning as a separate layer that doesn't dilute the reflection.

### Anti-Patterns

- No `---` horizontal rules between sections (they fragment the flow)
- No markdown tables for data (embed in prose)
- No "下個月要做的事" as numbered OKR list (fold into reflection)
- No forced metaphors or analogies (if it doesn't come naturally, don't force it)
- No AI-slop closings ("在這個快速變化的時代...")
- No em dashes

### Best For

- Monthly/quarterly personal retrospectives
- "What I shipped + what I learned" reviews
- Pieces where the real insight is self-discovery, not project updates
- When the author has data to share but the story matters more than the numbers

### Pairing Notes

- Combine with **Ping Chen techniques** for narrative flow: cross-domain one-liners, unresolved forks, physical/sensory grounding
- Combine with **Alan Chan techniques** for framework-as-spine when one theme dominates the month
- Data visualization cards (HTML → PNG) for social sharing of key metrics
